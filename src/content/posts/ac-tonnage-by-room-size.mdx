Picking an air conditioner starts with one question: how many tons? Tonnage is the unit that describes cooling capacity, and the fastest way to ballpark it is from the space you need to cool. Sizing AC tonnage by room size — or by whole-house square footage — gives you a starting number in seconds, and it is the relationship every homeowner and estimator reaches for first.

The catch is that square footage alone is a rough guide. It gets you close, but the real answer depends on your climate, your insulation, and how much sun and glass the space has. This guide covers the chart, the math behind it, and where the rule of thumb breaks down.

## What a Ton of Cooling Actually Means

A "ton" of air conditioning has nothing to do with weight. **One ton equals 12,000 BTU/hr of cooling capacity** — the heat-removal rate it once took to melt a ton of ice over 24 hours. So a 3-ton system removes about 36,000 BTU of heat per hour.

To turn a room or house into tons, you need to know how much heat it gains. The most common rule of thumb for cooling load is roughly **20 BTU per square foot** in a moderate climate. Combine the two facts and you get a clean shortcut:

**Tons = (square feet × 20 BTU/sq ft) ÷ 12,000**

Because 12,000 ÷ 20 = 600, that 20 BTU/sq ft figure works out to about **1 ton of AC per 600 square feet** in a mild climate, tightening toward **1 ton per 400 square feet** in hot regions. That spread — roughly 400 to 600 sq ft per ton — is the heart of every ac tonnage to square footage chart.

## The Tons-Per-Square-Foot Formula

The chart is just the formula rounded to standard equipment sizes. To run it for any space:

**Tons = (sq ft × BTU/sq ft) ÷ 12,000**

For a 1,600 sq ft home in a moderate climate at 20 BTU/sq ft:

**Step 1 — Find the load:** 1,600 × 20 = 32,000 BTU/hr
**Step 2 — Convert to tons:** 32,000 ÷ 12,000 ≈ 2.7 tons
**Step 3 — Round to a real size:** 2.7 rounds up to a **3-ton** unit

This is the ac ton calculation for room size in its simplest form. The only variable that really moves the answer is the BTU/sq ft figure, and that is set by climate. ## How Climate Changes the BTU per Square Foot

The single "20 BTU/sq ft" number is an average. The right size of ac unit per square foot shifts with your climate zone:

- **Northern / cooler climates:** about **18 BTU/sq ft**. Milder summers and shorter cooling seasons mean less heat to remove — closer to 1 ton per 650–700 sq ft.
- **Moderate / mixed climates:** about **20 BTU/sq ft**, the chart default — roughly 1 ton per 600 sq ft.
- **Hot, humid South:** about **22–25 BTU/sq ft**. Higher design temperatures plus the latent load from humidity push the requirement up.
- **Very hot, sunny desert regions:** **25–30 BTU/sq ft**. Intense solar gain and extreme design temperatures can shrink coverage to about 1 ton per 400–450 sq ft.

So the same 1,600 sq ft home that needs a 3-ton unit in a moderate climate might get by with 2.5 tons up north, while a sun-baked house in Phoenix could need a full 4 tons. Climate is the biggest lever on the answer, which is why a national rule of thumb can only ever be a starting point.

## Worked Example: A Single 400 sq ft Room

Say you want to cool one 400 sq ft room. Run the same math:

**400 × 20 = 8,000 BTU/hr → 8,000 ÷ 12,000 ≈ 0.67 ton**

That room needs about two-thirds of a ton, or roughly 8,000 BTU/hr. In practice you rarely install a tiny dedicated central unit for a single room — central systems are sized for the whole house and ducted to distribute that capacity. The exception is a **ductless mini-split**, where an individual head genuinely is sized per room. For a 400 sq ft room, a 9,000 BTU/hr (¾-ton) mini-split head is a common match. For window or portable units, you would size to that same ~8,000 BTU/hr figure.

This is why "ac tonnage by room size" really means two different things: sizing a *whole house* by total square footage, or sizing a *single zone* like a mini-split or window unit. The 20 BTU/sq ft math drives both — only the equipment differs.

## Adjustment Factors That Change the Answer

Two homes with identical square footage can need different tonnage. These factors push the load up or down:

- **Ceiling height.** The 20 BTU/sq ft rule assumes 8-foot ceilings. Vaulted or 10-foot ceilings add volume and heat gain — bump the load up.
- **Sun exposure.** West- and south-facing rooms with afternoon sun gain far more heat than shaded, north-facing spaces.
- **Window area and type.** Lots of glass, single-pane windows, or skylights raise the load. Low-E double-pane glazing lowers it.
- **Insulation and air sealing.** A tight, well-insulated home holds conditioned air; a drafty older home leaks it and needs more capacity.
- **Occupancy.** Each person adds roughly 400–600 BTU/hr of heat. Busy living spaces run hotter than spare bedrooms.
- **Kitchens.** Ovens, ranges, and refrigerators dump significant heat, so kitchens typically need extra capacity.

Each of these can swing the requirement by 10% or more. Stack a few in the same direction — a sunny, high-ceilinged room full of windows — and the square-footage estimate can be off by a half-ton or more.

## Why Rules of Thumb Aren't Enough: Manual J

Square-footage rules are quick, but they routinely mis-size systems because they ignore everything in the list above. The industry-standard fix is a **Manual J load calculation** — the ACCA method that totals heat gain room by room using your actual windows, insulation, orientation, infiltration, and local design temperatures.

The temptation is to "play it safe" and round up to a bigger unit. With air conditioning, bigger is usually worse:

- **Short-cycling.** An oversized AC cools the air to setpoint fast, then shuts off, then restarts minutes later. The constant stop-start wears out the compressor and wastes energy.
- **Poor dehumidification.** Removing humidity takes *run time*. An oversized unit satisfies the thermostat before it has run long enough to wring moisture out of the air, leaving the house cold but clammy.
- **Uneven temperatures and comfort complaints.** Short bursts of cooling never give the air time to mix and distribute evenly.

Undersizing has the opposite failure mode — the unit runs nonstop and still can't keep up on the hottest days. The right size is the one that matches the calculated load, which is exactly what Manual J delivers and what a rule of thumb only approximates. ## FAQ

### How many tons of AC do I need per square foot?

Plan on about 1 ton for every 400 to 600 square feet, depending on climate. The 20 BTU/sq ft rule of thumb lands near 1 ton per 600 sq ft in a moderate climate, tightening to roughly 1 ton per 400 sq ft in hot, sunny regions. Run the numbers through a tons-per-square-foot calculator and then confirm with a Manual J load calculation.

### What size AC for a 1,500 sq ft house?

A 1,500 sq ft house typically needs about a 2.5-ton system in a moderate climate (1,500 × 20 = 30,000 BTU/hr ÷ 12,000 = 2.5 tons). In a hot, humid or sunny climate it can rise to 3 tons, while a tight, well-insulated home up north might do fine with 2 tons. Adjustment factors like ceiling height, sun, and insulation decide which way to round.

### How many square feet does 1 ton of AC cover?

Roughly 400 to 600 square feet per ton. Use about 600 sq ft per ton in mild climates, 500 in moderate ones, and 400 to 450 in very hot or sunny regions. The exact coverage drops as climate, sun exposure, and ceiling height increase the cooling load.

### Is it better to oversize or undersize an AC?

Neither — match the load. An oversized AC short-cycles, dehumidifies poorly, and wears out faster, leaving the house cold but humid. An undersized unit runs nonstop and can't keep up on peak days. The correct size comes from a Manual J calculation, not from rounding a square-footage estimate up "to be safe."

### Why doesn't square footage alone give the right tonnage?

Square footage ignores the factors that drive real heat gain: insulation, air sealing, window area and orientation, ceiling height, sun exposure, occupancy, and local climate. Two identical-size homes can differ by a half-ton or more once those are counted. That's why square-footage rules are a starting point and a Manual J load calculation is the accurate method.
